<h2>Chinese Cleavers — for Everyday Prep</h2>

<p>The Chinese cleaver looks intimidating but is actually one of the most versatile knives in any kitchen. It's the only knife in most home kitchens across China and South-East Asia, and once you've used one for a week you'll understand why.</p>

<p>What it's <em>not</em> for: bone-in proteins or anything frozen. The thin edge will chip. Use a Japanese meat cleaver for those jobs.</p>

<h2>Japanese Meat Cleaver vs Western Cleaver — What's the Difference?</h2>

<p>Western butcher cleavers (the heavy square-bladed type used in old-school butcher shops) are bigger, heavier, and ground at a less acute angle than Japanese meat cleavers. They're built to chop through hard bone — beef joints, pork ribs, the kind of work commercial butchers do all day.</p>

<p>Japanese meat cleavers like the Kiba and Tetsuya sit between Western cleavers and chef knives — heavier than a Gyuto, lighter than a butcher's cleaver, with a sharper edge geometry that lets them slice cleanly as well as chop. For home use they're the better choice unless you're regularly working through full primal cuts.</p>

<h2>How to Care For a Cleaver</h2>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Hand-wash and dry immediately.</strong> Especially Chinese cleavers — the broad face holds water and stains.</li>
  <li><strong>Don't put cleavers in the dishwasher.</strong> Heat warps handles and dulls the edge.</li>
  <li><strong>Use the right knife for the job.</strong> Chinese cleavers chip on bone; Japanese meat cleavers can damage glass cutting boards.</li>
  <li><strong>Sharpen on a whetstone every 6–12 months.</strong> A 1000-grit stone is enough for most maintenance. Heavier cleavers benefit from a slightly steeper sharpening angle (~20°) to protect the edge during chopping.</li>
  <li><strong>Store in the supplied sheath or on a magnetic strip.</strong> Loose in a drawer, the heavy blade dulls itself bumping against other utensils.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>What's the difference between a Chinese cleaver and a meat cleaver?</h3>

<p>A Chinese cleaver (cai dao) is thin-bladed and used for everyday slicing, dicing, and vegetable prep — it's not for bones. A Japanese or Western meat cleaver is much heavier, with a thick spine designed to chop through bone and break down whole proteins.</p>

<h3>Can I use a Chinese cleaver for everything?</h3>

<p>For everyday prep, yes — slicing, dicing, mincing, even crushing garlic with the broad face of the blade. Don't use it on hard bone, frozen food, or anything that needs sawing — the thin edge will chip.</p>

<h3>Is a meat cleaver actually good for cutting meat?</h3>

<p>Yes — a meat cleaver is purpose-built for it. The weight and thick spine let you split joints and break down bone-in proteins that would damage a chef's knife. For boneless slicing, a Gyuto or Sujihiki is faster and cleaner.</p>

<h3>How heavy should a meat cleaver be?</h3>

<p>Most home meat cleavers sit between 350g and 600g. Lighter than that and you don't have the chopping mass to power through bone; heavier than that and your wrist will hate you after ten minutes of prep.</p>

<h3>How do I sharpen a cleaver?</h3>

<p>Same way as any Japanese knife — use a whetstone (1000/3000 grit combo for routine maintenance) and follow the existing bevel angle. Heavier cleavers benefit from a slightly steeper angle (around 20°) than a chef's knife to protect the edge during chopping.</p>

<h3>Are these cleavers dishwasher safe?</h3>

<p>No. The heat warps handles, the detergent dulls the edge, and contact with other utensils chips the blade. Hand-wash and dry immediately, every time.</p>
